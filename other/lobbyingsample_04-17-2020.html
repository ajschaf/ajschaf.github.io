This week's Middle East lobbying updates

Friday, April 17, 2020

By Aaron Schaffer (@aaronjschaffer | aschaffer@al-monitor.com)
PRESENTED BY

Welcome to Al-Monitor’s lobbying newsletter, where we bring you all the latest developments in Middle East lobbying in Washington.

Qatar Foundation hires PR firm RF|Binder

The Doha-based nonprofit Qatar Foundation has hired its first PR firm since the Ogilvy Group stopped working for it in July. Memac Ogilvy & Mather, which has a separate agreement with the foundation, signed a one-year, $360,000 contract with New York-based communications and consulting firm RF|Binder on March 31. The agreement, which was effective April 1, calls for the firm to develop PR pitches and promote the foundation’s work, conduct meeting onboarding and media planning, coordinate speaking opportunities for the foundation’s senior leadership and media trips to Qatar and develop an annual plan.

 

RF|Binder CEO Amy Binder and nine employees plan to work on the account. They include executive managing director William McBride; managing director Kelly Cameron; head of corporate media Karina Frayter; Jennifer Bergman; Julia Klein; Megan Manning; William Maroni; Serra Saridereli and Amee Upadhyay.

 

The Qatar Foundation has long been scrutinized by groups critical of Qatari influence in the United States. US universities have reported at least $50 million in monetary gifts from the foundation since 2013, according to an Al-Monitor review of Department of Education records. Of that, 97.9% was contributed to Texas A&M University, which has a branch in Qatar and, according to The Washington Post, has its Qatar expenses paid by the foundation.

Coronavirus stymies Libyan interior minister’s delegation to DC

In March, Libya lobbyist Mercury tried to get meetings with a half dozen lawmakers for a visit by Libyan Interior Minister Fathi Bashagha, who was planning to visit Washington “to discuss support from the Trump Administration and Congress for the Government of Libya, and to encourage appropriate condemnation of [Libyan strongman Khalifa Hifter's] ongoing assault on the Libyan people, infrastructure and oil industry,” according to a lobbying filing by Mercury. But that visit was stymied by the outbreak of the novel coronavirus, Al-Monitor has learned.

 

“Unfortunately due to the travel restrictions in the social distancing guidelines, the trip has been postponed,” Mohammed Ali Abdallah, the Libyan Government of National Accord's adviser for US affairs, told Al-Monitor. The visit was scheduled for a critical time — nearly a month after Bashagha publicly called for the United States to build a military base in Libya to push back against Russian influence in the country.

New contracts

  • Houston-based Bridge Builder Communications is being paid $25,000 per month to provide consulting services for Qatar Embassy activities in Florida. The firm’s founder and principal, George Smalley, is registered on the account, which originally aimed to support the embassy’s charitable gift programs including the Qatar Harvey Fund. Smalley, who has disclosed distributing press releases for Qatar, notably filed his Oct. 2018 proposal to Allison Biggs, the president of Lexington Avenue Consulting who also works for Qatar. In that proposal, Smalley listed his experience working for Saudi Arabia’s former Energy Minister Ali al-Naimi in the 1990s and living through Hurricane Harvey as helpful for the job, which involved communications support for a Texas visit by Qatar’s ambassador to the United States, Meshal bin Hamad Al Thani. “Presumably we'll need a message point expressing empathy,” Smalley wrote, “and the summary of impacts can provide memorable examples of the devastation for the Ambassador to reference. I lived through Harvey so crafting content to express empathy and take note of our resiliency will come naturally to me.” The Abu Dhabi Investment Authority and FTI Consulting have renewed their contract for three years effective March 4. The firm will continue to be paid $48,950 per month for PR services.
  • UAE’s search engine optimization contract is second most lucrative MENA contract since January 2019: Syracuse, New York-based search engine optimization firm Terakeet has signed the Middle East’s second most lucrative lobbying and PR contract since the beginning of 2019, when it contracted with the United Arab Emirates’ embassy in Washington early this year, newly released filings show. Terakeet and the UAE Embassy’s deputy chief of mission, Shaima Gargash, signed the one-year, $2.4 million contract in March. The contract is second in value only to Saudi petrochemicals giant SABIC’s $6.6 million contract with Edelman (including expenses) that began in February. We reported on the contract last month.

People on the move

  • Syracuse, New York-based search engine optimization firm Terakeet’s CEO, MacLaren Cummings, CTO Patrick Danial and vice president for account management Shannon Reedy as well as Gabriela Alise, Anneliese Harrison, Ryan Hurlburt, Antoinette Kimball, Brianna MacBlane, Kenneth Schiefer and Curtis Weyant have registered to work on the firm’s $2.4 million account with the UAE.
  • Quinn Emanuel partner Bill Burck stopped working as a foreign agent on the firm’s account with Kuwait-based logistics giant Agility Public Warehousing Company on Feb. 29. Burck, a Republican who has represented Secretary of State Mike Pompeo and numerous ex-Donald Trump administration officials, continues to provide legal services to the company that are exempt from disclosure to the Justice Department.
  • Edelman has added two employees and one consultant to its account with Saudi petrochemicals giant SABIC. Vice president Darcy Kohn and Benjamin Noel Stokes plan to work on the account in addition to Argentina-based consultant Maria del Pilar Conci, who will be paid $12,500. Conci previously coordinated communications for the 2018 G-20 summit in Argentina. Early this year, Edelman signed a $6 million contract with SABIC to promote the Business 20 (B20) dialogue forum, which Riyadh plans to host this year along with the G-20, with the international business community.
  • Holland and Knight has brought on Michigan City, Indiana-based consultant Tim Smith to its account with Kuwaiti businessman Talal al-Dabbous. Smith will be paid $300 per hour.
  • Sukanti Ghosh stopped working for APCO Worldwide and its client, the Bahrain Economic Development Board, on Oct. 11. Ghosh is currently a senior vice president at the Albright Stonebridge Group.

Latest filings

  • Qatar spy plane company Barzan Aeronautical continued its outreach to local officials in South Carolina and the state’s members of Congress in the six-month period ending Feb. 29. The company received more than $14 million from its parent company, Barzan Holdings, which is owned by Qatar’s Defense Ministry, and disclosed meetings with Charleston mayor John Tecklenburg, Charleston County Aviation Authority CEO Paul Campbell, South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster and aides to Sen. Lindsey Graham, R-S.C. Two officials who work for Barzan reported going on trips to Germany, Greece, Qatar, Turkey and the United Kingdom.
  • In the six-month period ending Feb. 29, Holland and Knight received $74,000 from nine Indian guest workers imprisoned in Saudi Arabia; $46,000 from Israel; $34,000 from Kuwaiti businessman Talal al-Dabbous and $0 from Qatar. Of those clients, the firm only reported lobbying for the nine Indian guest workers, who are Bohra Shiite Muslims; in its filing, Holland and Knight wrote that they’re been imprisoned “for religious freedom matters.” Scott Mason, a former campaign and transition staffer for US President Donald Trump who is the sole lobbyist on their account, met with an aide to Sen. Chris Coons, D-Del., and contacted the Tom Lantos Human Rights Commission, the State Department’s International Religious Freedom Office and the offices of Rep. Gus Bilirakis, R-Fla., Rep. Ken Calvert, R-Calif., Sen. Lindsey Graham, R-S.C. and Sen. Todd Young, R-Ind. A Coons spokesman told Al-Monitor that his office “has not taken any additional actions on the particular issue raised by Holland and Knight as of yet.”
  • The Washington Strategy Group, a lobbying firm run by former Deputy Assistant Secretary of State for Legislative Affairs Joel Rubin, received $60,000 from Iraqi Sunni Sheikh Jamal al-Dhari and his Iraq Stability and Security Program in the six-month period ending Feb. 29. Rubin disclosed meeting with acting Deputy Assistant Secretary of State for Iraq Rob Waller; Sen. Chris Murphy, D-Conn.; Rep. Raja Krishnamoorthi, D-Ill.; Rep. David Trone, D-Md.; and Rep. Jan Schakowsky, D-Ill., in December. Rubin previously disclosed sending requests to those lawmakers’ offices for meetings with al-Dhari. He did not respond to a request for comment on whether al-Dhari attended the meetings.
  • The Nineveh Plain Defense Fund, which works to support the armed Nineveh Plain Protection Units and the Iraqi political party that founded the units, the Assyrian Democratic Movement, lobbied the White House, Defense Department and State Department in the six-month period ending Feb. 29. At the time of the meetings, which occurred in September, October and February, the group “sought to explore the possibility for greater US engagement and support for the [Nineveh Plain Protection Units] within its broader security strategy within Iraq” and distributed “confidential briefing materials.”
  • Husch Blackwell Strategies, which lobbies for Qatar, landed a Sept. 23 meeting with Rep. Steve Chabot, R-Ohio, just four days after Chabot’s re-election committee received a $1,000 contribution from the firm’s CEO, Gregg Hartley. It was the only meeting with a member of Congress disclosed by the firm during the six-month period ending Feb. 29. Chabot has been critical of Qatar in the past; in March 2018, he signed on to a letter urging the Justice Department to investigate Qatar-based media outlet Al Jazeera, and he has co-sponsored legislation calling out the country for its support for Hamas. Neither Hartley nor Chabot’s office responded to requests for comment. Husch Blackwell Strategies disclosed receiving $150,000 from Qatar in the six-month period ending Feb. 29.
  • Kuwait-based logistics giant Agility Public Warehousing Company paid law firm Quinn Emanuel $333,000 in fees and expenses in the six-month period ending Feb. 29. Kristin Tahler, a partner at the firm, met with Rep. Don Beyer, D-Va. on Oct. 21 and met with more than 50 congressional aides from various offices over six months. Tahler and an associate at Quinn Emanuel, Marc Hedrich, also met with reporter Aaron Gregg, who writes about defense contractors for The Washington Post, on Oct. 21.
  • Bahrain’s Economic Development Board paid APCO Worldwide $66,000 in the six-month period ending Feb. 29. In October, the firm arranged meetings for two companies with the board in Washington. In addition, the firm contacted 17 outlets, including Politico, The Hill, Wired and gaming publications to “promote the country as an attractive location for business investment and expansion,” and met with Politico on Dec. 9.
  • The Palestine Monetary Authority paid DLA Piper $407,000 in fees and expenses in the six-month period ending Feb. 29. During that time period, lobbyists working on the account emailed and called David Peyman, the deputy assistant secretary of state for counter threat finance and sanctions, and Ryan Anderson, the chief of staff for Rep. Rashida Tlaib, D-Mich. (Tlaib is the first woman of Palestinian descent in Congress). DLA Piper reported no activity or fees for Bahrain, which it also represents. Lastly, the firm emailed Taylor Ruggles, the director of the State Department’s office of economic sanctions policy and implementation, about “matters regarding petroleum and petroleum products transiting Iran” for its client, Azerbaijan Railways. (DLA Piper received $176,000 in fees and expenses from the state-owned railway company.)

Press releases

  • Turkey’s opposition Republican People's Party (CHP) distributed English- and Turkish-language materials relating to the Turkish government’s response to the novel coronavirus outbreak, including criticism of the response and a CHP proposal to fight the virus.
  • Mercury Public Affairs, which lobbies for Libya’s Government of National Accord, is pushing back on what it calls a “false narrative” that its client is made up of — and supports — the Muslim Brotherhood. Mercury also distributed a Dec. 2016 Treasury Department press release on US sanctions on Syrian entities including Cham Wings Airlines, which Libya has accused of ferrying foreign fighters into its territory. (The airline has denied the allegations.) Two Mercury lobbyists also distributed articles in The Guardian and the Daily Signal on foreign interference in Libya, and the firm distributed a Washington Institute for Near East Policy article on Russia’s interests in Libya.

Arms sales

The State Department approved one Middle East arms sale on April 14.

  • Morocco: 10 anti-ship missiles ($62 million)

2020 watch

  • Liberal advocacy group J Street made its first ever presidential endorsement today, saying former Vice President Joe Biden should be elected president later this year. Biden has embraced the endorsement from the group, which spent $400,000 on lobbying in 2019.

This week in think tank funding

  • The Brookings Institution released its list of funders for the second half of 2019 this week. Qatar’s embassy remained a major contributor, giving between $250,000 and $500,000 — the think tank’s fourth-highest contribution tier. Brookings notably has an overseas center in Doha and has been the subject of scrutiny by right-wing groups for its ties to the emirate.

Caught our eye

  • The head of advertising giant Publicis is warning of an unprecedented ad-spending pullback as the novel coronavirus continues to impact the world. Saudi Arabia has paid Qorvis, a Publicis subsidiary, more than $35 million in fees and expenses since it was acquired by the conglomerate in 2014.
  • Washington-based lobbying firm JJ&B, which represents President David Granger of the oil-rich South American nation Guyana, distributed a dossier to congressional staffers calling out three men with Libyan, American and Austrian passports who were seen at a hotel in Guyana just before March 2 elections. The same page of the dossier calls out Mercury lobbyist Gustavo Arnavat, who represents the opposition People’s Progressive Party (PPP) for also being at the hotel. The presence of the three men, the dossier says, points “to possible involvement of foreign actors in some aspect of our electoral process.” (The election remains contested.) Mercury did not respond to a request for comment. In addition to the PPP, the firm notably represents Libya’s Government of National Accord, which has alleged Russian interference.
  • Abu Dhabi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Zayed pitched UAE ally and Libyan strongman Khalifa Hifter to members of then-President-elect Donald Trump’s foreign policy team at a meeting in New York in December 2016, The New York Times reports. Houston-based Linden Government Solutions represents Hifter; Mercury Public Affairs, Gerstman Schwartz and Gotham Government Relations work for the Government of National Accord, which Hifter is fighting against, and its affiliated entities.
  • Vox profiled Sen. Chris Murphy, D-Conn., who has established himself as a commanding foreign policy voice in the Democratic Party.
